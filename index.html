First Steps
"Build the balance and ankle strength to stand, glide, and stop"
Ankle stability Two-foot balance Hip strength Skating posture

Basic Skills 1 and 2 test your ability to stand, march, glide, dip, swizzle, wiggle, and do a snowplow stop. Everything here is about getting comfortable with your own bodyweight in an unstable position. This phase builds the ankle strength, balance, and knee bend that make those early on-ice skills possible. 3 sets of each exercise unless noted.

Equipment This Phase

Just a yoga mat, a smooth floor section (for sock-sliding drills), and a sturdy wall or chair for balance support. No weights needed ‚Äî this phase is entirely about movement quality and body awareness.

Weekly Schedule ‚Äî Repeat 2‚Äì3 weeks, increasing hold times by 5 seconds each week
Monday ‚Äî Ankle Strength & Standing Balance
Warm-Up
5 min: march in place, ankle circles (both directions, both feet), gentle knee bends
Exercise 1
Two-foot calf raises ‚Äî hold wall, rise onto toes slowly, lower slowly. 15 reps. This builds the push-off strength used in Basic 1's forward march and calf raises train the exact toe-to-heel blade rocking you'll do on the ice.
Exercise 2
Single-leg stand ‚Äî eyes open ‚Äî hold wall lightly, lift one foot 2 inches off floor, hold 10 seconds each side. 3 rounds. Basic 2's one-foot glide requires sustained balance on a single skate ‚Äî start here.
Exercise 3
Skater dip ‚Äî feet hip-width, bend knees and hips into a gentle dip (like a mini-squat), hold 3 seconds, stand. 10 reps. Mirrors the "dip" tested directly in Basic 1 ‚Äî learning to go low without tipping forward.
Exercise 4
Heel-to-toe straight-line walk ‚Äî walk a straight line placing each foot directly in front of the other, heel touching toe. 3 lengths of the room. Trains the narrow, controlled stance of skating glides.
Cool-Down
5 min: seated calf stretch, ankle circles, gentle quad stretch holding wall
Tuesday ‚Äî Core, Posture & Skating Stance
Warm-Up
5 min: arm circles, torso rotations, cat-cow stretches on hands and knees
Exercise 1
Skater stance hold ‚Äî feet hip-width, bend knees, lean slightly forward from hips (not waist), arms out to sides in a gentle oval. Hold 20 seconds. This is the actual on-ice ready position ‚Äî your body needs to memorize it before you put on skates.
Exercise 2
Wall angels ‚Äî stand with back, head, and arms flat on wall. Slowly slide arms up and down like a snow angel. 10 reps. Fixes the rounded shoulders that plague new skaters and trains the upright back posture required from Basic 1 onward.
Exercise 3
Knee plank hold ‚Äî on forearms and knees, hold a straight line from knees to head. 20 sec. Builds the deep core that keeps you upright when balance is challenged on the ice.
Exercise 4
Glute bridge ‚Äî lie on back, feet flat, lift hips until body is straight. Hold 3 sec, lower. 12 reps. The glutes power every skating push ‚Äî waking them up now makes forward stroking (Basic 3) feel natural later.
Cool-Down
Child's pose, chest opener lying on floor, gentle neck stretches
Wednesday ‚Äî Swizzle & Glide Simulation
Warm-Up
5 min light movement, joint circles from ankles to hips
Drill 1
Forward swizzle simulation (socks on smooth floor) ‚Äî stand with heels together, toes out. Push feet apart and squeeze back together in a V pattern, gliding forward. 6‚Äì8 reps in a row. This IS the Basic 1 forward swizzle ‚Äî just without ice. Feel the V-in, V-out motion.
Drill 2
Backward wiggle simulation (socks on smooth floor) ‚Äî feet parallel, wiggle hips side to side to propel yourself backward slowly. 6‚Äì8 wiggles. Maps directly to the Basic 1 backward wiggle ‚Äî the first backward movement you'll learn on ice.
Drill 3
Two-foot glide hold ‚Äî in skater stance, shift weight onto both feet, hold perfectly still for 5 seconds. Repeat 5 times. Practices the balance required to glide on two feet without panicking (Basic 1 core skill).
Drill 4
Snowplow stop simulation ‚Äî walk forward a few steps, then turn toes in and press heels out (pigeon-toed wedge) to "stop." 8 reps. The snowplow stop in Basic 1 uses this exact foot shape ‚Äî your legs need to know the feeling.
Stretch
Hip flexor lunge (30 sec each side), inner thigh butterfly stretch (60 sec), ankle alphabet
Thursday ‚Äî Hip Strength & Single-Leg Work
Warm-Up
5 min: side leg swings, hip circles, bodyweight squats √ó 10
Exercise 1
Side-lying hip abduction ‚Äî lie on side, lift top leg to hip height, hold 3 sec, lower. 12 each leg. The hip abductors hold you steady on one leg during glides ‚Äî this is one of the most important skating muscles to develop early.
Exercise 2
Bodyweight squat ‚Äî feet hip-width, sit back as if into a chair, stand. 10 reps. Focus on keeping your chest up and knees tracking over your second toe. The bent-knee position of skating lives here.
Exercise 3
Single-leg stand ‚Äî eyes closed ‚Äî attempt to hold balance on one foot with eyes closed for as long as possible. Track your record. Eyes-closed balance directly trains the proprioceptive system ‚Äî the same system that keeps you upright when the ice is moving under you.
Exercise 4
Rocking horse simulation ‚Äî step forward onto one foot, rock back onto the other in a rhythmic rocking motion. 2‚Äì3 reps per side. The "rocking horse" in Basic 1 is this exact motion ‚Äî learn the rhythm before you do it on blades.
Cool-Down
Seated hamstring stretch, figure-4 hip stretch, calf stretch on step

Phase 1 ‚Äî Key Exercises Explained
Skater Stance Hold
3 √ó 20 sec holds
The foundation of everything. Knees bent, slight forward lean from hips, arms out. Your body must memorize this before you step on ice ‚Äî it feels unnatural at first but becomes automatic with repetition.
Single-Leg Stand
3 √ó 10 sec each leg
Basic 2's one-foot glide happens on a single skate. This simple exercise starts building the hip and ankle stability to make that feel controlled rather than terrifying.
Forward Swizzle Simulation
3 √ó 6‚Äì8 reps
The swizzle is the first real propulsion skill in skating. Practicing the V-out, V-in foot pattern on a smooth floor in socks means your legs already know the shape when you're on blades.
Two-Foot Turn
3 √ó 8 reps
Basic 2 requires a forward-to-backward two-foot turn in place. Practicing this pivot on flat ground teaches the rotation without the fear of falling on ice.
Side-Lying Hip Abduction
3 √ó 12 each leg
The hip abductor muscles stabilize every single-leg glide in skating. They're chronically weak in beginners. Building them now prevents the wobbly, collapsing glides that hold most new skaters back.
Snowplow Stop Simulation
3 √ó 8 reps
The pigeon-toed wedge shape of the snowplow stop is unfamiliar to most people. Practicing it on land means your feet already know where to go when you need to stop on the ice for the first time.
Phase 1 ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • The single most important thing in this phase is ankle strength. If your ankles feel wobbly, spend extra time on calf raises and ankle circles every single day.
  • When practicing the skater stance, film yourself from the side. Most beginners lean too far forward from the waist rather than hinging from the hips ‚Äî these look similar but feel very different on ice.
  • The eyes-closed single-leg stand is your most important benchmark. Track your seconds every session ‚Äî improvement here directly predicts how quickly you'll feel comfortable on one skate.
  • Do the sock-floor drills on a genuinely smooth surface (hardwood, tile). If your socks grip the floor, you won't get the right sliding sensation.
  • Don't neglect flexibility ‚Äî tight hip flexors are the single biggest posture problem for new skaters. The 30-second hip flexor lunge every day will make a visible difference within two weeks.
02
Edges & Crossovers
"Develop forward stroking, edge control, and crossover mechanics"
Forward stroking Edge lean Crossover walk Forward 3-turns

Basic Skills 3 and 4 introduce the mechanics that define real skating: forward stroking with correct blade use, edges on a circle (inside and outside, both feet), forward crossovers, and the forward outside 3-turn. Off-ice you can't replicate the glide, but you can train the lean, the weight transfer, and the turning pattern that make these elements work.

Recommended New Equipment

A set of light resistance bands (~$12) is strongly recommended this phase for hip abduction work. Continue using a smooth floor section for edge and crossover simulations. A yoga mat for all floor exercises.

Monday ‚Äî Stroking Strength & Single-Leg Power
Warm-Up
8 min: dynamic leg swings, hip circles, 2 min march in place
Exercise 1
Step-touch stroking simulation ‚Äî in skater stance, step sideways onto one foot, hold the glide position for 2 seconds, step to the other side. 10 reps each direction. Mimics the push-and-glide rhythm of Basic 3 forward stroking ‚Äî the weight transfer and the pause are what matter.
Exercise 2
Single-leg squat with assist ‚Äî hold wall, lower on one leg until other foot lightly touches floor, stand back up. 8 each leg. Forward stroking is a series of single-leg loading moments ‚Äî this builds the quad strength to sustain them.
Exercise 3
Lateral band walks ‚Äî band just above knees, step sideways in a squat position, 10 steps each direction. 3 rounds. The hip abductors fire on every skating push. This is the most direct off-ice exercise for the muscles used in stroking and crossovers.
Exercise 4
Single-leg calf raise ‚Äî hold wall, rise onto one foot only. 12 each leg. The toe push that ends every skating stroke comes from single-leg calf strength ‚Äî now harder than the two-foot version.
Cool-Down
10 min lower body stretching, inner thighs and calves especially
Tuesday ‚Äî Edge Lean & Circle Mechanics
Warm-Up
5 min: ankle rolls, hip circles, skater stance hold √ó 3
Drill 1
Edge lean hold ‚Äî outside ‚Äî stand on one foot, lean the whole body (not just the hip) outward over the foot. Hold 5 seconds. 6 each foot. This is the body position of a forward outside edge ‚Äî the lean must come from the whole body, not just tipping sideways from the hip.
Drill 2
Edge lean hold ‚Äî inside ‚Äî stand on one foot, lean inward (toward the other foot). Hold 5 seconds. 6 each foot. The inside edge lean has a different feel ‚Äî more like leaning into a banked turn.
Drill 3
Circle walk with lean (socks on smooth floor) ‚Äî walk in a circle, leaning into the center on each step. 3 circles each direction. Simulates the constant lean of skating a circle on an edge.
Exercise
Full plank ‚Äî hold on toes (or knees if needed). 20‚Äì30 sec, 3 rounds. Core stability is what keeps your upper body quiet while your legs lean into an edge below ‚Äî the two have to work independently.
Cool-Down
Seated straddle stretch, hip flexor lunge with arm reach, spine twist
Wednesday ‚Äî Crossover Walk & Weight Transfer
Warm-Up
5 min: hip swings, leg crosses, gentle dynamic stretches
Drill 1
Crossover walk ‚Äî forward ‚Äî in a slow circle, step one foot across and in front of the other (not just beside), then step to the side to follow. 8 crossover steps each direction. This is the foot pattern of Basic 4's forward crossovers ‚Äî the cross-in-front step must go truly across, not just adjacent.
Drill 2
Crossover lean walk ‚Äî perform the crossover walk above while consciously leaning into the circle's center. The lean and the crossing step together produce the feeling of skating crossovers. 3 circles each direction.
Drill 3
Half swizzle pump simulation (socks) ‚Äî stand on one foot, do a mini V-out push with the free foot, recover it back in. 6‚Äì8 reps each foot. Maps to the Basic 3 half swizzle pumps on a circle ‚Äî single-foot propulsion for the first time.
Stretch
Pigeon pose (45 sec each), frog stretch (60 sec), inner thigh ‚Äî your hips need to be open for crossovers to feel fluid
Thursday ‚Äî Forward Outside 3-Turn Prep
Warm-Up

Drill 1
3-turn foot pattern ‚Äî stationary ‚Äî stand on one foot, rotate your whole body 180¬∞ (from forward to backward facing) using a toe pivot, land on the same foot still facing backward. Hold 3 seconds. 6 each foot. The 3-turn is a rotation on one foot from forward to backward ‚Äî this is that exact pattern without the glide.
Drill 2
Step into 3-turn ‚Äî take 2 steps forward, then perform the 3-turn pivot described above. 6 each foot. Adding the forward approach makes it feel more like the on-ice version.
Exercise
Dead bug ‚Äî lie on back, arms up, knees at 90¬∞. Lower one arm and opposite leg without touching floor, return. 8 each side. Core anti-rotation ‚Äî the same stability that keeps your upper body facing forward while your feet turn in a 3-turn.
Exercise
Two-foot spin simulation ‚Äî stand on both feet on a smooth surface, use your arms to wind up, then spin 360¬∞ in place. 5 reps. Basic 3 introduces the two-foot spin. Getting comfortable with dizziness and rotation on flat ground is valuable preparation.

Phase 2 ‚Äî Key Progressions
Edge Lean Hold
3 √ó 5 sec each side/foot
Edges are the foundation of all skating. The lean must come from the whole body over the skate blade ‚Äî not just a hip tilt. This drill teaches the full-body commitment that makes clean edges.
Crossover Walk
3 circles each direction
The foot pattern of crossovers feels deeply unnatural at first. Walking through it on land ‚Äî with no fear of falling ‚Äî lets your feet learn the crossing step before blades are involved.
3-Turn Pivot
3 √ó 6 each foot
The forward outside 3-turn (Basic 4) is many skaters' first real turning skill. The foot pivot simulation trains the rotation pattern and the landing balance before you're trying to do it on a moving blade.
Lateral Band Walk
3 √ó 10 steps each way
The hip abductors are the unsung engine of skating crossovers ‚Äî they hold you on the outside edge through every cross step. Banded lateral walks are the most direct off-ice exercise for this specific demand.
Two-Foot Spin Simulation
5 full rotations
Basic 3 introduces the two-foot spin. Getting comfortable with rotation and dizziness on flat ground ‚Äî including learning to spot or focus your gaze ‚Äî makes the on-ice spin far less disorienting.
Crossover Figure-Eight
4 complete figure-eights
Basic 4 requires two full figure-eights of crossovers. Practicing the connected circles ‚Äî with the lean switching at the center ‚Äî teaches the spatial pattern your body needs to replicate on ice.
Phase 2 ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • On edge lean holds, the biggest mistake is leaning just the hip outward (like a side stretch) rather than the whole body. Imagine the ice pushing up through your foot at an angle ‚Äî you lean against that angle as a whole unit.
  • In the crossover walk, the crossing foot must go genuinely in front, not just beside. Walk along a straight line on the floor ‚Äî the crossing foot should land on the other side of that line.
  • When practicing 3-turn pivots, keep your arms out for balance and focus on landing on the same foot you started on. The "trace" of the 3-turn on ice is a 3-shaped line ‚Äî your foot makes a turn on the spot, not a step.
  • The frog stretch and pigeon pose will feel increasingly important this phase. Tight inner thighs make crossovers cramped and awkward ‚Äî open hips make them effortless.
  • If you have access to ice time even just once a week, this is a great phase to start. Basic 3‚Äì4 skills are very teachable in early lessons, and your off-ice work will make a visible difference.
03
Backward Skating & First Jumps
"Master backward edges, the one-foot spin, and your first jump shapes"
Backward crossovers Forward inside 3-turn One-foot spin Bunny hop & spiral

Basic Skills 5 and 6 are where skating starts to look like figure skating. You'll add backward crossovers, the beginning one-foot spin, a hockey stop, the forward inside 3-turn, a bunny hop, a spiral, and a lunge. Off-ice work now targets spin balance, jump landing positions, and the backward mechanics that feel most foreign to new skaters.

Warm-Up
8 min: jog in place, dynamic leg swings, bodyweight squats √ó 12
Drill 1
Backward walking with control ‚Äî walk backward in a straight line, taking 12+ steps without looking behind you. 4 lengths. Backward skating requires comfort moving in the direction you cannot see ‚Äî this is the minimal first step to building that confidence.

Backward crossover walk ‚Äî walk backward in a circle using a crossover step pattern (crossing one foot behind the other). 3 circles each direction. Basic 5's backward crossovers use the same crossing pattern as forward, just in reverse ‚Äî the foot crosses behind rather than in front.

Forward inside 3-turn pivot ‚Äî stand on one foot leaning inward (inside edge position), rotate 180¬∞ to backward, land on the same foot still leaning inward. 6 each foot. Thursday ‚Äî Bunny Hop, Lunge & Spiral
Warm-Up
8 min: full warm-up including light jumping in place to prepare for impact
Drill 1
Bunny hop ‚Äî jump off one foot onto the toe of the same foot, then roll onto the flat. Land quietly. 10 reps each foot. The bunny hop in Basic 6 is the simplest jump in skating ‚Äî a small hop onto the toe pick, then flat. Practicing on flat ground removes the ice fear and teaches the toe-then-flat landing.
Drill 2
Forward lunge ‚Äî step forward into a deep lunge, extending the back leg fully with knee near the floor. Hold 3 sec, stand. 8 each leg. The Basic 6 forward lunge on ice requires a very deep extension ‚Äî your back flexibility and quad strength must both allow it.
Drill 3
Spiral hold without support ‚Äî stand freely, slowly lift one leg behind you as high as comfortable, hold for 10 sec. 4 each leg. Basic 6's forward spiral on a straight line is one of skating's most iconic positions ‚Äî this is it. The standing leg must stay straight and your chest stays up.
Drill 4
Landing position hold ‚Äî stand on one leg, free leg extended back, arms forward and back (skating landing position), hold 5 sec. 6 each leg. Every jump on ice ends in this position ‚Äî getting it familiar and stable off-ice means cleaner landings from the start.

4 √ó 10 sec each leg
The spiral on a straight line (Basic 6) is one of skating's most recognizable positions. Building it off-ice develops the back and hip strength ‚Äî and the flexibility ‚Äî to make it look effortless on the ice.

6 √ó 5 sec each leg
Every jump in skating ends in this position. Spending time holding it stably off-ice means that when you land a jump on ice for the first time, your body already knows where to go ‚Äî a genuine competitive advantage.

3 √ó 8 reps

Phase 3 ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • When practicing spiral holds, think about lengthening through your whole body ‚Äî reaching forward with your chest and reaching back through your heel, not just lifting your leg. The length of the body line is what judges evaluate.
  • For spin position balance, your standing knee should be very slightly bent, not locked. A locked knee is actually less stable than a gently soft one on ice.
  • The bunny hop on flat ground should land almost silently ‚Äî if you're making a loud thud, you're not absorbing enough through your ankle and knee. Quiet landings transfer directly to cleaner ice landings.
  • Backward crossover walks feel awkward for everyone. Don't rush them. Count the steps out loud to build the rhythm, then let the rhythm become automatic before adding any speed.
  • By this phase, if you haven't started ice time yet, this is the moment ‚Äî Basic 5‚Äì6 skills are meaningful enough to work on with a coach, and your off-ice foundation will make the learning curve noticeably gentler.
04
Mohawks, Jumps & Spins
"Master the Mohawk, waltz jump, and combination movement ‚Äî you're ready for ice"
Mohawk transitions Waltz jump One-foot upright spin Combination moves

Basic Skills 7 and 8 are where skating becomes athletic and expressive. The Mohawk is a two-foot turn between edges. The waltz jump is the first real rotational jump. The combination move in Basic 8 chains multiple skills together. Off-ice you can train all of these patterns, and by the end of this phase you'll have the strength, coordination, and body knowledge to pass the full Basic Skills sequence.

Deep hip stretches ‚Äî pigeon (90 sec), frog (90 sec). Mohawks demand the most hip openness of any Basic Skills element.

First Time on Ice
"Learn to stand, march, glide, and stop on blades"

Your first sessions on ice should feel like a reunion with movements you've already practiced off-ice. Don't rush to "look like a skater" ‚Äî every minute you spend getting comfortable at low speed pays dividends later. Use the boards freely. Falling is normal and expected; your off-ice work means you'll recover faster than a skater who came in cold.

üõº

Gear for This Phase

Rental skates are fine to start. Make sure the boot is laced firmly all the way to the top ‚Äî floppy ankles in a poorly laced boot is the most common cause of wobbling for new skaters. Wear thin socks, not thick ones. Helmet strongly recommended. Wrist guards optional but useful.

Session Structure ‚Äî Use for every on-ice session this phase (2‚Äì3 sessions per week ideal)
Part 1 ‚Äî Getting On & Standing (10 min)
Step 1
Step onto the ice holding the boards. Stand still. Feel the blade under your foot. Notice that the blade wants to slide ‚Äî resist it by slightly gripping the ice with your ankles. Take 30 seconds just to feel this.
Step 2
March in place at the boards. Lift each foot 2 inches, set it down. This is Basic 1's first element ‚Äî on ice it feels different because each footfall slides slightly. March 20 steps.
Step 3
March forward along the boards, one hand lightly touching. 3 lengths. Your off-ice march prepared you for this ‚Äî trust that your knees are already bent correctly.
Step 4
Two-foot glide. March 3 steps, then bring feet together and glide. Hold the glide until you stop. 5 repetitions. The glide will feel shorter than you expect ‚Äî that's normal. It gets longer as you gain confidence and speed.
Part 2 ‚Äî Basic 1 Elements (15 min)
Element 1
Dip. While gliding on two feet, bend your knees deeply and rise back up. 6 dips while moving. You practiced this as a stationary exercise off-ice ‚Äî now it happens on a moving glide. Keep your chest up.
Element 2
Forward swizzles. Start from standstill: heels together, toes out. Push feet apart and squeeze back together. 6‚Äì8 in a row. You've done this in socks ‚Äî on ice it's the same V-out, V-in motion, now with the blade actually gliding.
Element 3
Backward wiggles. Feet parallel, wiggle hips side-to-side to propel yourself slowly backward. 6‚Äì8 wiggles. Stay close to the boards on your first attempts. Look over your shoulder briefly to check your path.
Element 4
Snowplow stop. Push off into a two-foot glide, then turn toes in and push heels out to form a wedge and stop. 6 reps. Practice stopping before you skate faster ‚Äî your confidence on ice is directly tied to knowing you can stop.
Element 5
Rocking horse. Step forward onto one foot, rock weight back onto the other in a rhythm. 3 reps each side. This should feel familiar ‚Äî it's the same pattern you practiced in the off-ice Thursday session.
Part 3 ‚Äî Basic 2 Elements (15 min)
Element 1
Forward one-foot glides. Skate forward and lift one foot slightly off the ice, gliding on the other. Hold as long as you can, then switch. 6 each foot. This is the biggest step-up from Basic 1 ‚Äî all your single-leg balance work pays off here.
Element 2
Backward two-foot glide. Push off the boards, glide backward on two feet. 5 reps. Stay relaxed and keep knees bent ‚Äî the tendency is to straighten up and lean back when going backward, which causes falls.
Element 3
Backward swizzles. From backward two-foot glide: push feet apart, squeeze back together while moving backward. 6‚Äì8 in a row. The same V-motion as forward swizzles, now moving in reverse.
Element 4
Two-foot turn forward to backward in place. Glide forward, come to near-stop, pivot on two feet to face backward, hold for 2 seconds. 6 reps. You've practiced this pivot on flat ground ‚Äî on ice the blade can swivel more freely, which actually makes it easier.
Element 5
Moving snowplow stop. Skate forward with some speed, then apply the snowplow stop. The "moving" version requires committing to the wedge while actually gliding ‚Äî more confident than the static version.
Part 4 ‚Äî Free Practice & Cool-Down (10 min)
Free Skate
Skate around the rink freely at your own pace. No specific elements ‚Äî just move, glide, and enjoy being on the ice. This unstructured time is where the elements start to connect into actual skating.
Review
Before leaving the ice, pick the one element that felt hardest today. Skate it 3 more times with full focus. Ending a session by improving one specific thing builds momentum.
Cool-Down
Step off the ice, remove skates, and spend 5 minutes stretching calves, hip flexors, and inner thighs. The boots compress your feet and ankles ‚Äî releasing that immediately after skating is important for recovery.
Phase 1 On-Ice ‚Äî What to Focus On
Knee Bend
Always ‚Äî every element
The most common mistake for first-time skaters is straightening the knees. Bent knees lower your center of gravity, give you shock absorption, and make every element easier. If something feels wrong, the first fix is always "bend your knees more."
Eyes Forward
Always ‚Äî never look at your feet
Looking down at your blades tilts your body forward and causes falls. Your feet know what to do ‚Äî trust the off-ice training. Keep your gaze on the boards ahead of you, at eye level.
One-Foot Glide
6 √ó each foot per session
The one-foot glide is the gateway to all of Basic 2 and the foundation of everything after. Spend extra time here. Even holding it for 2‚Äì3 seconds is progress ‚Äî each session it will get longer.
Snowplow Stop
Practice every session
You cannot skate confidently until you can stop reliably. Practice your snowplow stop at the beginning of every session, before doing anything else, so it becomes automatic. Knowing you can stop lets you skate faster.
Falling Safely
Know before you need it
When you feel yourself falling, bend your knees, crouch low, and fall to the side onto your hip and thigh ‚Äî not backward onto your tailbone or forward onto your hands. Getting up: roll to your side, get on your hands and knees, step one foot up, push up.
T-Position Start
Use for every push-off
Before every push from standing, place your feet in a T-shape (one foot forward, one foot perpendicular behind it). Push off the back foot's inside edge. This is the correct way to start from a standstill and prevents the "penguin waddle" start.
Phase 1 On-Ice ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • Your first session will feel nothing like what you practiced off-ice ‚Äî and that's fine. The ice is a new sensory environment. Give yourself 2‚Äì3 sessions just to get comfortable before judging your progress.
  • Rental skates are often dull and poorly fitted. If you find yourself after 3‚Äì4 sessions and still feel like the blades are slipping sideways uncontrollably, ask the rink to sharpen the rentals you're using ‚Äî it makes a dramatic difference.
  • The backward wiggle feels silly but is genuinely important ‚Äî it's the only backward propulsion available to you at this stage. Don't skip it in favor of trying to look cooler.
  • Two-foot glide time is your best early metric. Note at the end of each session how long your best glide felt. When it starts lasting 3‚Äì4 seconds, you're ready to add Basic 3 elements.
  • If a rink near you offers a Basic Skills group lesson, even one or two sessions with an instructor will accelerate your progress significantly ‚Äî especially for the one-foot glide and snowplow stop technique.

Stroking, Edges & Crossovers
"Start skating with real push, lean into your first edges, and cross your feet"
Basic 3 Basic 4 Forward stroking Edges on a circle Forward crossovers Forward outside 3-turn

This is where skating starts to feel like skating. Forward stroking, edges, and crossovers transform your movement from a careful shuffle into something fluid. The forward outside 3-turn is your first real turning skill. Your off-ice edge lean work and crossover walks have prepared your body ‚Äî trust that foundation and focus on the ice feeling of each element.

Consider Your Own Skates

By this phase, if you're skating 2‚Äì3 times per week, investing in your own skates is worthwhile. A properly fitted boot makes an enormous difference for edge work ‚Äî you need to feel the blade tilt clearly through the boot. Entry-level figure skates ($80‚Äì$150) are far better than broken-down rentals.

Session Structure ‚Äî 2‚Äì3 sessions per week; each session follows this arc
Warm-Up Lap ‚Äî Basics 1‚Äì2 Review (10 min)
Lap 1
Easy forward skating around the rink ‚Äî no pushing yet, just gliding on two feet, getting your bearings and warming up your ankles.
Lap 2
Forward one-foot glides ‚Äî alternate feet every few seconds. Hold each glide a beat longer than feels comfortable. These should now feel significantly steadier than your Phase 1 first session.
Lap 3
Forward swizzles the full length of the rink ‚Äî 6‚Äì8 in a row. Then backward swizzles back. Use this lap to get the blade feeling under your feet before adding new elements.
Basic 3 ‚Äî Forward Stroking & Half-Swizzle Pumps (15 min)
Element 1
Forward stroking with correct blade use. Push off one foot's inside edge, glide on the other foot, then push off that foot's inside edge. The push comes from the whole blade, not just the toe. Arms out, knees bent, chin up. 4 full laps of the rink.
Element 2
Half-swizzle pumps on a circle. Skate a circle using one foot as the gliding foot ‚Äî push off and pull back in with the other foot in a pumping action. 6‚Äì8 pumps clockwise, then counterclockwise. This is single-foot propulsion for the first time ‚Äî the building block of stroking power.
Element 3
Two-foot spin. Skate forward, step onto both feet on an inside edge circle, use your arms to wind up, then pull them in tight to spin. Up to 2 revolutions. Your off-ice two-foot spin simulation prepared you for the rotation ‚Äî on ice the blades pivot on the ice more smoothly.
Element 4
Forward slalom. Skate in a weaving S-curve pattern, shifting your weight from one outside edge to the other. This is edge-leaning in a flowing pattern ‚Äî the most fun way to practice edge transitions early on.
Basic 4 ‚Äî Edges & Crossovers (15 min)
Element 1
Forward outside edge on a circle. Skate a circle on one foot, leaning outward (to the outside of the circle). The lean comes from your whole body, not just your hip. 4‚Äì6 half-circles each foot. The body position you practiced off-ice ‚Äî this is it on a moving blade.
Element 2
Forward inside edge on a circle. Same circle pattern but leaning inward (toward the center of the circle). 4‚Äì6 half-circles each foot. The inside edge lean feels more like leaning into a banked turn ‚Äî commit to it rather than tipping cautiously.
Element 3
Forward crossovers. On a circle, step the outside foot across and in front of the inside foot, then step the inside foot to the side. 4‚Äì8 crossovers per circle, two full figure-eights each direction. Your off-ice crossover walk gave you the foot pattern ‚Äî now it happens on a leaning circle with momentum.
Element 4
Forward outside 3-turn. Skate forward on an outside edge, then rotate your upper body in the direction of travel ‚Äî the blade will "turn" on the ice from forward to backward. Hold the backward glide. 3 reps each foot. The pivot you practiced off-ice now has a real glide entry and exit.
Cool-Down & Backward Stroking Intro (10 min)
Element
Backward stroking. From a backward two-foot glide, push off one foot's outside edge and glide backward on the other. This is one of Basic 4's elements ‚Äî the backward version of the forward push. 3 lengths. The push comes from the outside edge, which feels counterintuitive at first.
Element
Backward snowplow stop. While skating backward, push one heel outward to slow down. 6 reps, alternating feet. Basic 4's backward stop ‚Äî essential for feeling safe skating backward before adding speed.
Cool-Down
Easy skating lap around the rink at low speed. Off ice: 5 min stretch ‚Äî calves, hip flexors, inner thighs. Note one specific improvement from last session and one thing to work on next time.
Phase 2 On-Ice ‚Äî What to Focus On
The Push, Not the Step
Every stroking lap
New skaters often step sideways rather than pushing off a real edge. The push in forward stroking comes from the inside edge of the whole blade extending out to the side ‚Äî not from pushing the toe back. Feel the difference.
Whole-Body Edge Lean
All edge circle work
When you practiced edge leans off-ice, the instruction was to lean the whole body ‚Äî not just tilt the hip. On ice, this is even more important. If only your hip tilts, the blade flattens. The whole body leans as one unit over the blade.
Crossover: Cross In Front
All crossover figure-eights
The outside foot must cross genuinely in front of the inside foot ‚Äî not just step beside it. If you're not crossing far enough, the crossover produces no power. You practiced this on a floor line off-ice ‚Äî visualize that line on the ice surface.
3-Turn: Lead With the Upper Body
All 3-turn practice
The forward outside 3-turn is initiated by rotating the upper body and shoulders in the direction of travel ‚Äî the blade follows the body's rotation. If you try to turn the feet first, the turn feels forced. Lead with the chest, let the feet turn under you.
Free Foot Position
Glides and edges
During single-foot glides and edges, the free foot (the lifted one) should be held close to the skating ankle ‚Äî not dangling out or swinging. A controlled free foot makes every edge cleaner and is one of the first things judges look for.
T-Stop Introduction
Practice each session
Basic 4 includes no explicit stop, but the T-stop (introduced in Basic 6) is worth introducing early. Drag one foot behind you perpendicular to your skating direction ‚Äî the blade's flat side creates friction. A second stopping tool gives you more confidence at higher speeds.
Phase 2 On-Ice ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • Forward stroking is the most important skill to develop cleanly in this phase. Every other element ‚Äî edges, crossovers, spins, jumps ‚Äî is built on top of good stroking technique. Spend at least one full lap on stroking at the start of every session.
  • The 3-turn on ice will likely feel much easier than the off-ice pivot simulation, because the blade can actually swivel. The hard part is holding the backward glide after the turn without stepping out of it ‚Äî practice the hold.
  • For crossovers, skate slower than you think you need to. At low speed the foot pattern is clearer and easier to correct. Speed without technique just bakes bad habits in faster.
  • If you haven't started lessons yet, this phase is the most important time to do so. A coach watching your edge work live can identify a lean or weight-distribution problem in seconds that might take you months to self-diagnose.
  • After each session, log your session notes in the Progress Tracker. Which elements felt better than last time? Which still feel uncertain? This reflection accelerates learning.

Basic 5 and 6 are the phases that make skating feel like figure skating. You add backward crossovers, your first on-ice spin, a hockey stop, and your very first jumps ‚Äî the bunny hop, the lunge, and the spiral. Every one of these has a direct off-ice equivalent you've been training. The ice is where that preparation pays off.

By this phase, if you have your own skates, get them sharpened before starting. Dull blades make backward edges and the one-foot spin feel much harder than they should ‚Äî the blade should bite the ice cleanly when you apply pressure to an edge. Most pro shops sharpen for $10‚Äì$20.

Lap 1‚Äì2

Lap 3
Crossover figure-eights ‚Äî one full figure-eight each direction. Warm up the hip abductors and the lean pattern before attempting backward elements.
Lap 4
One forward outside 3-turn each foot, then one forward inside 3-turn each foot. Review all four turns before moving on.
Basic 5 ‚Äî Backward Edges & Crossovers (15 min)
Element 1
Backward outside edge on a circle. From a backward two-foot glide, lean outward to place weight on one foot's outside edge. Glide the circle. 4‚Äì6 half-circles each foot. The body leans away from the circle's center ‚Äî same lean you practiced in the off-ice backward edge hold.
Element 2
Backward inside edge on a circle. Lean inward (toward the circle's center) on the backward glide. 4‚Äì6 half-circles each foot. Backward inside edges are the platform for future jump landings ‚Äî establishing them cleanly now pays dividends later.
Element 3
Backward crossovers. On a circle, cross one foot behind the other (the opposite of forward crossovers ‚Äî behind, not in front) and push with the front foot's outside edge. 4‚Äì8 crossovers per circle, two figure-eights each direction. Your off-ice backward crossover walk prepared this foot pattern.

Hockey stop. Skate forward with confidence, then rotate both feet 90¬∞ sideways simultaneously while bending deeply ‚Äî both blades scrape to stop. 6 reps. Commit to the rotation ‚Äî a half-hearted hockey stop catches an edge and causes a fall.
Element 5
Side toe hops. Hop sideways from one foot to the other landing on the toe pick first, then flat. 8 hops each direction. Basic 5's toe hop ‚Äî your first use of the toe pick on ice.
Basic 6 ‚Äî Turns, First Jumps & Spiral (15 min)
Element 1
Forward inside 3-turn. Enter on a forward inside edge, rotate 180¬∞ to backward. 3 reps each foot. The entry edge is different from the outside version ‚Äî lean inward before initiating the turn, not outward.
Element 2
Bunny hop. Skate forward, step onto the toe pick of one foot, spring into the air, land on the same toe pick, roll to flat. 5 reps each foot. This is your first jump on ice. The off-ice version prepared the landing mechanics ‚Äî just add the toe-pick spring.
Element 3
Forward lunge. Skate forward, step deep into a lunge with one leg extended forward near the ice, hold while gliding, rise back up. 4 each leg. The blade of the lunging foot stays flat on the ice ‚Äî you're gliding on it while the back knee approaches the ice.
Element 4
Forward spiral on a straight line. Skate forward on one foot and lift the free leg behind you as high as possible while gliding in a straight line. Hold for the length of the ice. 3 each foot. Your off-ice spiral hold built the back and hip strength ‚Äî now you hold that position while balancing on a moving blade.
Element 5
T-stop. Drag one foot behind you perpendicular to your skating direction ‚Äî the blade creates friction to slow you. 6 reps each foot. Basic 6's introduced stop ‚Äî a more versatile tool than the snowplow at higher speeds.

Step 1
Two-foot spin review. 3 two-foot spins as a warm-up before attempting one-foot. Get your rotation feeling and your center of balance warmed up.
Step 2
One-foot spin from two-foot. Begin a two-foot spin, then lift the free foot into the spin position. Hold for up to 3 revolutions. Lifting the free foot during a spin is the core challenge of Basic 5 ‚Äî your off-ice spin balance drills prepared exactly this moment.
Step 3
Spin entry practice. Practice the forward inside Mohawk entry into a spin (the standard entry path): left forward outside edge ‚Üí right forward inside Mohawk ‚Üí left backward inside edge ‚Üí step to right forward inside edge ‚Üí spin. Don't rush the entry ‚Äî a good spin comes from a controlled entry, not a fast one.
Cool-Down
Easy laps, then off ice: calves, hips, and spine stretch. Spins put rotational stress on the spine ‚Äî take the cool-down seriously.
Phase 3 On-Ice ‚Äî What to Focus On
Backward Crossover Direction
Behind, not in front
The most common confusion with backward crossovers: the crossing foot goes behind the other foot (not in front, as in forward crossovers). The push comes from the front foot's outside edge. If your crossovers feel stiff, this distinction is usually why.
Hockey Stop: Commit
Every stop practice
A half-committed hockey stop is more dangerous than a full one ‚Äî if you only partially rotate your feet, one blade catches a forward edge and you fall. Commit fully to the rotation. Trust the blades to scrape.
Bunny Hop Landing
Every repetition
Land on the toe pick, then roll to flat. The "roll to flat" is what makes this a skating jump rather than just a hop ‚Äî it teaches the blade landing sequence used in all future jumps. Slow down and feel the toe-then-flat contact.
Spiral Free Leg Height
Every spiral attempt
For the Basic 6 test, the free leg in the spiral must be at or above hip height. This is where your off-ice spiral hold height directly translates. If it's below hip height on ice, increase the off-ice spiral hold work in your training.
Spin: Stay Over the Blade
All spin attempts
The most common spin problem is "traveling" ‚Äî moving across the ice rather than spinning in one spot. This happens when your weight drifts off the blade's spin point (the ball of the foot). Think about pressing down through the ball of the foot throughout the spin.
Forward Inside 3-Turn Exit
Each 3-turn attempt
After the forward inside 3-turn, you exit on a backward outside edge. Holding that exit edge ‚Äî not stepping out of it ‚Äî is what distinguishes a clean 3-turn from a scrambled one. Hold the exit for 2 full counts before moving on.
Phase 3 On-Ice ‚Äî Coaching Notes
  • Backward crossovers feel awkward for almost everyone at first. The hardest part is trusting the push from the front foot's outside edge ‚Äî it feels like pushing in the wrong direction. Slow down, exaggerate the push, and let the momentum build gradually.
  • For the spiral, skate faster before lifting the free leg ‚Äî more speed gives you more time to hold the position before the blade slows. At very slow speeds, the spiral is actually harder to hold.
  • The one-foot spin is one of the skills where a single lesson with a qualified instructor is worth many hours of self-directed practice. Spin technique has nuances ‚Äî entry edge, centering point, arm pull ‚Äî that are very difficult to self-diagnose.
  • If the hockey stop is proving difficult, make sure you're starting the rotation while you still have adequate speed. Trying to hockey stop from a very slow speed is actually harder because there's not enough momentum to carry the rotation through.
  • This phase is a natural milestone to schedule a test prep session or informal review with a Basic Skills instructor. You're getting close to the Basic 5‚Äì6 test standard.
